Pneumonia and Consolidation: Radiographic Reference Notes

Consolidation on a chest radiograph typically appears as a region of increased
opacity where air in the alveoli has been replaced by fluid, pus, blood, or
cells. When consolidation is accompanied by air bronchograms, infectious
pneumonia should be high on the differential. Lobar pneumonia classically
presents as a well-defined opacity confined to one lobe, while
bronchopneumonia tends to appear as patchy, multifocal opacities.

Suggested next steps when pneumonia is suspected from imaging alone:
- Correlate with clinical symptoms: fever, productive cough, pleuritic chest
  pain, and hypoxia.
- Obtain a complete blood count and inflammatory markers (CRP, procalcitonin)
  to support or rule out an infectious process.
- Consider sputum culture or blood cultures in moderate-to-severe cases
  before starting empiric antibiotics.
- Reassess with a follow-up radiograph in four to six weeks after treatment
  to confirm resolution, particularly in patients over 50 or with risk
  factors for underlying malignancy.
- In immunocompromised patients, consider a broader differential including
  atypical organisms and fungal infection, and have a lower threshold for CT
  imaging.

Differentiating pneumonia from other causes of opacity:
- Pulmonary edema tends to be bilateral, gravity-dependent, and associated
  with cardiomegaly and vascular redistribution, whereas infectious
  consolidation is often more focal.
- Atelectasis can mimic consolidation but usually shows volume loss (shifted
  fissures, elevated hemidiaphragm) rather than air bronchograms.
- Malignancy should be considered when consolidation fails to resolve after
  an appropriate course of antibiotics.
