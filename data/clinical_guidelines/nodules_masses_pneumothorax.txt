Pulmonary Nodules, Masses, and Pneumothorax: Radiographic Reference Notes

A pulmonary nodule is generally defined as a rounded opacity up to 3
centimeters in diameter, while lesions larger than 3 centimeters are termed
masses and carry a substantially higher likelihood of malignancy. Key
features to describe include size, border characteristics (smooth,
lobulated, or spiculated), presence of calcification, and growth on serial
imaging.

Suggested next steps when a nodule or mass is flagged:
- Compare against prior imaging whenever available; stability over two or
  more years is reassuring for benignity.
- For indeterminate nodules, follow established size-based follow-up
  protocols (such as Fleischner Society guidelines) to determine the
  interval for repeat CT imaging.
- Spiculated margins, irregular borders, or associated lymphadenopathy
  should prompt referral for CT chest and consideration of biopsy.
- Review smoking history and other risk factors to help stratify
  malignancy risk.

Pneumothorax appears as a visceral pleural line with absent lung markings
peripheral to that line. It should be assessed for size and for tension
physiology (mediastinal shift, flattened diaphragm), which is a medical
emergency requiring immediate decompression.

Suggested next steps when pneumothorax is flagged:
- Assess clinical stability immediately; any signs of tension pneumothorax
  require urgent needle decompression or chest tube placement.
- Estimate the size of the pneumothorax to guide management between
  observation, needle aspiration, or chest tube insertion.
- Identify and treat any underlying cause, such as recent trauma, chest
  procedure, or underlying bullous lung disease.
- Obtain a follow-up film after intervention to confirm lung re-expansion.
