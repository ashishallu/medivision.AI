Cardiomegaly and Pleural Effusion: Radiographic Reference Notes

Cardiomegaly on a frontal chest radiograph is commonly screened for using
the cardiothoracic ratio, where a ratio greater than 0.5 (heart width
divided by widest thoracic diameter) is suggestive of an enlarged cardiac
silhouette. This finding alone does not identify the underlying cause and
should always be correlated with clinical history and, where indicated,
echocardiography.

Suggested next steps when cardiomegaly is flagged:
- Review prior imaging if available to determine whether the finding is new
  or chronic and stable.
- Correlate with signs and symptoms of heart failure: dyspnea, orthopnea,
  peripheral edema, and elevated jugular venous pressure.
- Order or review a BNP or NT-proBNP level and an ECG.
- Recommend echocardiography to assess chamber size, ejection fraction, and
  valvular function.

Pleural effusion appears as blunting of the costophrenic angle on an upright
film, and larger effusions produce a meniscus sign. Bilateral effusions in
the presence of cardiomegaly are frequently transudative and related to
heart failure, while unilateral or exudative-appearing effusions warrant
broader workup.

Suggested next steps when effusion is flagged:
- Assess for associated cardiomegaly, which raises suspicion for a
  cardiogenic transudative effusion.
- Consider diagnostic thoracentesis when the effusion is new, large, or the
  etiology is unclear, sending fluid for Light's criteria to distinguish
  transudate from exudate.
- In the absence of cardiac findings, consider infectious, malignant, or
  inflammatory causes and correlate with clinical presentation.
